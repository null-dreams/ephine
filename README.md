# Mender

## Introduction

**Mender** is a satellite settlement connected to the the village of Bushben in the Western Farmlands Region of the Asteria Kingdom. Situated on the fertile banks of the river Sioor, Mender is a small rural community with a population of **32 people**, whose livelihoods are primarily sustained through fishing and agriculture. The settlement derives its name from its location along a prominent meander of the Sioor River. Although modest in size, Mender occupies an important economic position within the local region due to its long-standing specialization in harvesting the tiger-scaled fish, a species native to the Sioor and regarded as a delicacy throughout the surrounding settlements.

Mender lies approximately five kilometers from Bushben and is separated from it by extensive cultivated farmland, irrigation channels, and managed fishing ponds. The settlement maintains close economic and social ties with Bushben, relying on the village for trade, administration, specialized goods, and broader connections to the markets of the Asteria Kingdom. In return, Mender supplies fish, agricultural produce, and river resources to the surrounding communities.

The settlement is composed primarily of several extended family households, with much of its social and economic activity centered around family cooperation and communal labor. Due to its small population, most residents perform multiple occupations throughout the year, alternating between fishing, farming, maintenance of fish ponds, and seasonal agricultural work. Despite having experienced significant population decline in previous decades, Mender remains a stable and self-sustaining community. Its residents are generally regarded by neighboring settlements as hardworking, practical, and dependable people, though also somewhat conservative in their customs and methods. Daily life in Mender is characterized by close interpersonal relationships, strong family ties, and a reliance on established traditions passed down through generations.

As one of the three satellite settlements associated with Bushben, Mender represents the primary fishing center of the local region and continues to maintain its reputation as the foremost producer of tiger-scaled fish along the Sioor River.

## History

### Founding (Year 910)

The settlement of **Mender** was founded in the year **910** by **Boran Sinoy**, the then family head of the Sinoy fishing family of Bushben.

At the time, the **tiger-scaled fish** native to the **Sioor River** had begun gaining popularity as a local delicacy. However, the fish's exceptionally durable scales made proper processing difficult. Improper filleting often damaged the skin, resulting in rapid spoilage of the meat.

Boran Sinoy discovered a method of filleting the tiger-scaled fish without damaging its scales or skin, greatly improving its preservation and marketability. He subsequently taught the technique to members of the Sinoy family. As demand for tiger-scaled fish increased, the Sinoy family decided to relocate closer to the Sioor River to dedicate more family members to fishing and processing activities. This relocation marked the establishment of Mender.

### Early Growth (Years 911–946)

The commercial success of the Sinoy family attracted additional settlers. Two other fishing families subsequently established residences in Mender, transforming the settlement into a prosperous fishing community.

During this period, Mender developed:

* specialized fishing methods for tiger-scaled fish,
* fish preservation techniques,
* expanded fishing ponds,
* stronger trade relations with Bushben.

The settlement experienced steady population growth and increasing prosperity.

### Peak Prosperity (Year 947)

By the year **947**, Mender had reached a population of approximately **100 residents**. This period represented the height of the settlement's prosperity. Fishing innovations were frequent, and much of the technical expertise regarding the harvesting, processing, and preservation of tiger-scaled fish was maintained by the elder members of the community.

The economy of Mender was heavily dependent on fishing, though supplementary agriculture was also practiced.

### Population Collapse (Year 961)

Beginning in the year **961**, Mender experienced a severe and unexplained decline in population. Over a relatively short period, approximately half of the settlement's population perished. The mortality rate was significantly higher among older residents. The inhabitants of Mender never determined the cause of the sickness. Various explanations circulated within the community, though none achieved widespread acceptance.

The loss of the elder population had long-lasting effects on the settlement. The elders had served as:

* innovators,
* instructors,
* organizers,
* repositories of fishing knowledge.

Their deaths resulted in the loss of substantial technical and practical expertise.

### Decline and Stagnation (Years 962–975)

Following the sickness, the surviving population adopted increasingly conservative approaches to fishing and agriculture.\
Experimentation and innovation largely ceased, as the younger generation favored established and predictable methods of sustaining themselves. Although Mender remained economically viable, its growth stagnated and its influence declined.

### Migration to Bushben (Years 976–984)

Beginning around the year **976**, younger residents increasingly sought employment opportunities outside the settlement.

Approximately **27 residents** relocated to Bushben to pursue work primarily as merchant apprentices and laborers.

As of the current year, **984**, Mender's population has declined to **32 residents**. Despite its reduced size, Mender remains the principal producer of tiger-scaled fish in the Bushben region and continues to maintain a stable trade relationship with its parent village.

## Population

As of the year 984, Mender has a population of 32 human inhabitants. The settlement is composed primarily of several interconnected family units, with the Sinoy, Nush, and Katum family lines having historically played the most significant roles in the development of the community.

Due to its small population, social life in Mender is highly familial in nature. Most residents are related either by blood or marriage, and interpersonal relationships form the basis of the settlement's economic and social organization. Knowledge and occupations are traditionally passed down through family lines rather than through formal education.

The population of Mender reached its historical peak of approximately 100 residents in the year 947. Following the sickness of 961 and subsequent migration to Bushben, the settlement experienced a prolonged population decline, stabilizing at its current population during the late 970s.

### The Sinoy Family

The Sinoy family is regarded as the founding family of Mender and remains one of its most respected family lines.

The family traces its prominence to **Boran Sinoy**, who founded Mender in the year 910 after discovering a method of properly processing tiger-scaled fish without damaging their scales. Boran's innovations established the economic foundation upon which the settlement was built.

Following Boran's death in 925, leadership of the fishing enterprise passed primarily to his eldest son, **Ruan Sinoy**, who oversaw much of Mender's period of prosperity. Ruan died during the sickness of 961.

Three of the Sinoy brothers survived the sickness, likely due to their unusually high consumption of fish and comparatively low consumption of locally cultivated fruit. The surviving branches of the Sinoy family continue to operate much of Mender's fishing industry.

Among the notable members of the Sinoy family was **Maen Sinoy**, the youngest brother of Ruan Sinoy, who married **Bini Nush** and contributed to the development of Mender's fishing and food preparation traditions. Maen died during the sickness.

### The Nush Family

The Nush family historically specialized in the preparation, preservation, and culinary development of tiger-scaled fish.

The most notable members of the family were the sisters **Bini Nush** and **Boni Nush**, who, beginning around the year 925, developed numerous innovations in filleting, preservation, and cooking techniques. Their work significantly improved both the quality and marketability of tiger-scaled fish and later formed the foundation of Mender's trade relationship with Kiribat.

**Bini Nush**, who later married Maen Sinoy, remains one of the oldest living residents of Mender. Approaching eighty years of age, she is among the few surviving individuals who personally witnessed the settlement's founding generation and period of prosperity. Despite suffering from chronic breathing ailments following the sickness, she continues to serve as one of the principal repositories of Mender's oral history and culinary traditions.

**Boni Nush** married a travelling merchant and spent much of her adult life outside Mender. Having no descendants, she represents the only surviving branch of the Nush family not permanently settled within the community.

The descendants of Bini Nush continue to maintain many of the food preparation traditions developed by their ancestors.

### The Katum Family

The Katum family historically served as the commercial and mercantile foundation of Mender.

Its most influential member, **Hin Katum**, was instrumental in transforming Mender from a local fishing settlement into a prosperous regional supplier of tiger-scaled fish. In the year 943, Hin successfully negotiated a commercial agreement with the workers' union of Kiribat, establishing a trade relationship that would sustain Mender's prosperity for decades.

Although Hin achieved considerable financial success, he remained personally attached to Mender and continued to live much as the other residents did. He died during the sickness of 961 at the age of sixty-three.

Two of Hin's younger siblings survived the sickness, having relocated to Kiribat in the year 950 following Hin's commercial success. Their descendants remain residents of Kiribat and maintain limited contact with Mender.



Today, the people of Mender largely regard the prosperity of the settlement's early years as the result of the efforts of the Sinoy, Nush, and Katum families. The memory of these families continues to influence the social and cultural identity of the settlement.

## Economy

The economy of Mender is characterized by a combination of subsistence agriculture, specialized fishing, barter-based local exchange, and limited participation in the monetary economy of the Asteria Kingdom. Although the settlement historically maintained a relatively high currency inflow for its size, its present economy is largely oriented toward self-sustenance and risk minimization.

Historically, Mender's monthly currency inflow averaged approximately **50 Boten**, primarily through the sale of tiger-scaled fish to the nearby town of Kiribat. Following the sickness of 961 and the subsequent decline in population and productivity, this figure gradually decreased. As of the year 984, the settlement's total monthly currency inflow is estimated to range between **5 and 10 Boten**, with the majority of monetary transactions being conducted by members of the Sinoy and Katum families.

Unlike larger settlements, Mender operates primarily through a barter economy. Currency plays a limited role in daily life and is principally reserved for the payment of taxes and the acquisition of goods unavailable through local exchange networks.

### Agricultural Production

Agriculture serves primarily as a means of household sustenance rather than commercial profit.

The principal crops cultivated in Mender are:

* **Khum**, the most widely bartered crop in the settlement,
* **Rici**, a staple crop cultivated by several families,
* **Cati**, grown primarily by the Nush family.

The Sinoy family maintains three crop fields, cultivating primarily Rici and Khum to support their own household consumption. The Nush family owns two crop fields, producing Rici and Cati, with much of the agricultural labor performed by seasonal wandering laborers.

Agricultural land ownership ultimately resides under the authority of the village of Bushben, with Mender functioning as a satellite settlement within Bushben's administrative jurisdiction.

### Fishing Economy

Fishing remains the principal economic activity of Mender and constitutes the settlement's primary source of external trade.

The Sinoy family continues to dominate the fishing industry, owning five fishing ponds as well as numerous family-owned boats and fishing nets inherited through several generations. Two of the ponds are currently maintained primarily by the elder members of the family, while the remaining ponds are operated by the working-age generation.

The Nush family maintains two additional fishing ponds and continues to contribute to the processing and preparation traditions historically associated with tiger-scaled fish.

Although Mender's fishing methods have remained largely unchanged since the sickness of 961, the settlement retains its reputation as the principal producer of tiger-scaled fish along the Sioor River.

### Trade and Currency

Mender participates in two distinct economic systems.

Internally, most exchanges occur through direct barter. Fish, crops, labor, repair work, and household goods are routinely exchanged without the use of currency.

Externally, the settlement participates in the monetary economy through trade with Kiribat and neighboring settlements.

Historically, the Katum family served as the principal commercial intermediaries of Mender. The commercial agreements established by Hin Katum during the settlement's period of prosperity allowed tiger-scaled fish to be sold in the markets of Kiribat, generating significant currency inflow for the settlement.

At present, members of the Sinoy family continue to rely upon the Katum family and their extended relatives in Kiribat to facilitate the sale of fish and agricultural products. In addition, the Katum family maintains a large merchant stall in Bushben, second in prominence only to that of the Figin family.

The Katum family also derives supplemental income through woodworking and repair services, including the maintenance of boats, piers, roofs, and other village structures.

### Taxation and Economic Behavior

The tax system of the Asteria Kingdom only permits taxation on realized monetary value. Consequently, residents of Mender must periodically convert goods into currency in order to satisfy their tax obligations.

Failure to pay taxes does not immediately result in criminal punishment. Instead, repeated non-payment results in escalating administrative and military harassment by regional authorities, creating strong incentives for households to maintain at least a minimal level of monetary income.

This system has significantly influenced the economic culture of Mender. Following the sickness of 961, surviving residents increasingly avoided experimentation and economic risk, preferring established methods of production that guaranteed predictable income sufficient to support taxation and household subsistence.

As a result, Mender's economy in the present day is characterized by stability, self-sufficiency, and a pronounced aversion to unnecessary economic risk.
