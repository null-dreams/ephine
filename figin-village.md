# Figin Village

## Introduction

Figin is a satellite settlement associated with the village of Bushben in the Western Farmlands Region of the Asteria Kingdom. Located approximately 12 kilometers west of Bushben within one of the largest clearings of the Altin Forest, Figin is a specialized craft settlement with a population of 40 people. Unlike the other satellite settlements of Bushben, Figin's economy and social organization are centered almost entirely around a single extended family and their traditional crafts.

The settlement was established in the year 826 by Dowan Figin, a highly skilled smith and merchant who relocated his family from Bushben following disputes regarding taxation and economic autonomy. Since its founding, Figin has remained under the administrative jurisdiction of Bushben while maintaining a high degree of economic independence.

Figin is widely recognized throughout the surrounding region for its production of metal wares, woodworking, and specialized industrial craftsmanship. Its workshops manufacture a diverse range of products, including household utensils, agricultural implements, construction materials, transportation equipment, and custom military armaments. During periods of regional conflict, the settlement has historically supplied weapons and armor to larger markets through its commercial connections.

The settlement occupies a strategically advantageous location within the Altin Forest. The surrounding woodland provides abundant firewood and grazing areas, while a large pond situated at the center of the settlement supplies water essential for metalworking and daily life. The nearby Sioor River, located approximately five kilometers west of Figin, provides access to overland trade routes connecting the settlement to the town of Chokum and other regional markets.

In contrast to the modest residential structures inhabited by its residents, Figin's industrial facilities are among the most substantial structures in the local region. The settlement contains three large workshops constructed with brick walls and stone foundations, reflecting generations of accumulated expertise in fire prevention and industrial construction. These workshops are situated along the reinforced banks of the central pond and are equipped with piers, rope pulley systems, storage facilities, and dedicated work areas for both metalworking and woodworking.

The Figin family maintains a longstanding cultural tradition emphasizing craftsmanship, self-sufficiency, and disciplined labor. Although the settlement has historically generated significant wealth through trade, military contracts, and specialized manufacturing, its residents generally favor modest personal lifestyles and regard practical skill and hard work as the primary sources of social prestige.

Due to deliberate population management practices established during the early history of the settlement, Figin has maintained a relatively stable population throughout much of its existence. The settlement's continued prosperity has been sustained through regional trade networks, intermarriage with neighboring communities, and the systematic transfer of technical knowledge between generations.

Today, Figin remains the principal center of metalworking and industrial craftsmanship within the Bushben sphere of influence and continues to maintain extensive commercial relationships with Bushben, Kiribat, Chokum, and regional markets throughout the Asteria Kingdom.

## History

### Founding of Figin (Year 826)

The settlement of Figin was established in the year **826** by **Dowan Figin**, then the head of the Figin family and one of the most accomplished smiths of the Bushben region. Dowan was widely regarded as a highly charismatic and exceptionally skilled craftsman whose reputation extended beyond Bushben and into neighboring towns.

Despite achieving considerable financial success through his craft, Dowan became increasingly dissatisfied with what he perceived to be unfair taxation and social treatment under the administration of Bushben. Believing that his family would prosper more under their own management, Dowan relocated his family to a large clearing within the Altin Forest, approximately twelve kilometers west of Bushben.

Utilizing his existing clientele and commercial connections, Dowan secured rights to the land and established Figin as an independent family settlement. However, rather than seek direct administrative recognition from the kingdom, Dowan negotiated an arrangement with the then village chief of Bushben whereby Figin would remain administratively attached to Bushben while retaining substantial internal autonomy. This arrangement exempted the family from many administrative obligations while preserving access to regional trade networks.

At the time of its establishment, the Figin family consisted of twelve members.

### The Expansion Period (Years 843–860)

After serving as family head for seventeen years, Dowan retired in the year **843** at the age of fifty-two and transferred leadership to his eldest son, **Radn Figin**, then twenty-seven years old.

Influenced by his father's ambition and commercial philosophy, Radn and his brothers sought to expand the family's business beyond the Bushben region. During this period, the family established regular trade relations with the towns of **Kiribat** and **Chokum**, significantly expanding their commercial reach.

Although Radn himself married a woman from Bushben, one of the most consequential events in Figin's history involved his younger sister, **Shiya Figin**. During a visit to Chokum accompanying her brothers, Shiya encountered the influential merchant **Kant Hume**. Following a period of courtship and investigation by her brothers into Kant's character and business standing, Shiya agreed to marry him in the year **854**.

The marriage proved to be one of the most significant developments in the history of the Figin family. Through Kant Hume, the family gained access to one of the wealthiest travelling merchant associations in the kingdom.

Recognizing the opportunities presented by these new connections, the retired Dowan Figin traveled to the Royal Capital in the company of his son-in-law. During his stay, Dowan became convinced that the family should transition from an informal family enterprise into a formally recognized commercial organization. Through the support and guarantees provided by the merchant association, the Figin family was officially registered as a recognized business within the kingdom's records in the year **860**.

During this same period, several members of the Figin family established marital ties with prominent families from Chokum and Litten, further expanding the family's commercial and social network.

### The Division of Craft (Years 877–897)

Following Radn's retirement, leadership of the family passed not to his eldest son, **Wan Figin**, but rather to **Kom Figin**, the son of Radn's younger brother.

Wan Figin, while respected within the family, lacked the physical strength required to participate in heavy smithing work. Unwilling to see his son excluded from the family enterprise, Radn encouraged the development of woodworking as an extension of the family's traditional business. This initiative was supported by several women who had married into the family and who had not been raised within smithing households.

Over time, woodworking became a permanent and important component of the Figin economy, producing furniture, carts, boats, construction materials, and household goods.

Despite this success, tensions emerged between Wan Figin and Kom Figin. Kom, who deeply respected the traditions of metalworking, regarded Wan's inability to practice smithing as a personal weakness and considered him unworthy of inheriting leadership of the family. This disagreement produced a prolonged period of internal family conflict lasting nearly two decades.

Kom's tenure as family head ended following a severe accident sustained while working at the forge. Having also lost his eldest son in a mining accident in the year **883**, Kom retired from leadership and transferred authority to his second son, **Bowa Figin**, in the year **897**.

### The Consolidation Period (Years 897–934)

The leadership of **Bowa Figin** marked one of the longest and most influential periods in the history of the settlement.

Recognizing the risks posed by uncontrolled population growth within a family settlement, Bowa introduced a number of reforms intended to preserve the prosperity and stability of Figin. Most notably, he established a policy limiting each couple to a maximum of two children and prohibited marriage between close relatives. These policies encouraged marriages with neighboring settlements and towns while preventing the population from exceeding the settlement's economic and residential capacity.

Under Bowa's leadership, the family expanded its commercial network through strategic marriages, merchant partnerships, and increased regional trade. Family members were regularly dispatched to mines near Chokum to acquire practical mining experience and establish additional commercial contacts.

Bowa remained family head for thirty-seven years before retiring in the year **934**, making his tenure the longest in the history of Figin.

### The Meritocratic Period (Years 934–958)

Following Bowa's retirement, leadership of the Figin family passed to his eldest daughter, **Rini Figin**, becoming the first woman in the family's history to assume the position of family head.

Rini had demonstrated exceptional aptitude in both metalworking and woodworking and was widely regarded as more skilled than many of her male relatives. Although some elder family members initially objected to her succession, Rini rapidly established herself as an effective and capable leader.

Rini later married **Rowan Alti**, a member of the influential Alti merchant family. Although Rowan was considered the least favored member of his own family, his integration into the Figin family brought additional commercial contacts and strengthened existing trade networks.

Rini and Rowan had two children, **Koum Figin** and **Kiri Figin**.

### The Modern Period (Years 958–Present)

In the year **958**, leadership of the Figin family was jointly transferred to Koum and Kiri Figin, marking another unprecedented development in the history of the settlement.

Although **Kiri Figin** formally serves as the principal family head, **Koum Figin** acts as her chief advisor and oversees many of the family's academic, administrative, and strategic affairs.

Kiri continued the family's tradition of technical specialization, including service in the mining regions near Chokum, while Koum became the first member of the family to pursue formal scholarly and academic interests.

Both siblings entered marriages that further expanded the family's social and commercial networks. Kiri married a commoner from Litten, while Koum entered into a strategic marriage with **Lini Roum**, the daughter of a merchant family from the Royal Capital.

As of the year **984**, Koum and Kiri continue to oversee the affairs of Figin. However, leadership is widely expected to pass in the near future to **Chora Figin**, grandson of Bowa Figin, who has recently established successful commercial agreements with additional settlements located further downstream of the Sioor River.

Over its one hundred and fifty-eight years of existence, Figin has evolved from a family of twelve displaced craftsmen into one of the most influential industrial and commercial families within the Western Farmlands Region of the Asteria Kingdom.
